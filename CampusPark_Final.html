01 · Problem Definition & Stakeholders
Why campus parking needs a fix.
The Problems
01
Parking on campus is difficult
SDSU has 35,000+ students competing for ~2,590 spots across 8 structures. With no unified real-time system, students have no way to know where space is available before they even leave home.
02
Students waste time searching
Drivers circle structure levels or visit multiple lots before finding a spot. During peak hours (8–10am), this can burn 15–20 minutes per person, every single school day.
03
Causes stress and lateness
Arriving late to class due to parking uncertainty is a recurring stressor for commuter students. It affects academic performance, raises anxiety, and erodes the campus experience.
04
Increases traffic congestion
Uninformed drivers looping through structures and campus roads create unnecessary vehicle volume, slowing down everyone and worsening bottlenecks on College Ave and Montezuma Rd.
Stakeholders
ST
Students
Primary users · highest impact
Both commuters and on-campus residents. Commuters need real-time availability before they leave home. Residential students use community-specific alerts and the crowd-report feature to keep data fresh for peers.
FS
Faculty & Staff
Secondary users · permit holders
Professors and staff hold dedicated permits but still face lot closures during events, maintenance, or permit zone changes. They benefit from proactive alerts and reliable structure-level status before commuting to campus.
DV
Developers (Our Team)
CS 250 team · proxy users
All 4 team members are SDSU students who park on campus. We are not building for a hypothetical user. We experience the problem ourselves every week, which means requirements come directly from lived frustration, not assumptions.
02 · Our Solution
CampusPark: the app that ends the guessing.
A React Native mobile app built specifically for SDSU that gives students and faculty real-time parking intelligence, powered by the campus community itself.
APP
Mobile App for Real-Time Parking
CampusPark shows live availability across all 8 SDSU parking structures with color-coded status: green (open), yellow (filling), red (full). Students select their campus and residential community at login, so the home screen surfaces the lots most relevant to them. No more blind guessing before you leave the house.
Live spot counts 8 structures React Native
RPT
Crowd-Sourced User Reports
When a student arrives or departs, they submit a one-tap report. These crowd-sourced updates flow into the Python/Flask REST API backend, which recalculates availability in real time. The more students use it, the more accurate it gets. A full report history lets users track their own contributions and review past sessions.
Community-driven REST API History log
UX
Simple and Fast Interface
Designed for speed: the home screen loads structure cards instantly, the report modal takes two taps, and smart push alerts notify users before they even start their commute. Settings let users customize their notification preferences per structure. The interface is clean, readable in sunlight, and requires zero learning curve.
Push alerts 2-tap reports Zero friction

02 · What We Learned
Requirements from three lenses.
Actual Needs
What real users told us they needed, not what we assumed.
Pre-trip data, not on-arrival
Users don't want to know parking is full after they've driven there. The app must surface availability before they leave home.
Community-scoped alerts
Residential students don't need alerts for every structure. They need alerts for the two or three closest to where they live.
One-tap reporting
Users will only submit crowd reports if it takes seconds. Any more friction and they skip it, breaking the data loop.
Workflow Understanding
How the parking decision actually plays out, step by step.
Decision happens before departure
Students decide which structure to target while still at home or en route, not in the lot. The app must be fast to open and instantly readable.
Visual status over numbers
In observations, students glanced at their phones while moving. Color + bar was faster to process than reading a spot count number.
Alerts and history serve different moments
Alerts are consumed passively, before a trip. History is reviewed intentionally, after the fact. Separate tabs, separate UX patterns.
Internal Constraints
Limits that shaped scope and technical decisions.
No access to SDSU sensor data
No API or feed from SDSU's parking system. All real-time data must come from crowd reports. This made the reporting flow a core feature, not a nice-to-have.
Mock data as placeholder
Backend not yet connected to frontend at MVP stage. TypeScript mock arrays used to validate UX before live data was wired in.
4-person team, shared workload
Each feature was evaluated against team capacity. If it couldn't be built well in scope, it was moved to To Do on the Kanban board rather than cut entirely.
03 · Development Process
How we built with Kanban.
Continuous development with a visual task board. No fixed sprints, just steady progress through a shared To Do / In Progress / Done workflow.
Why Kanban?
As a 4-person student team, fixed sprint deadlines would have added overhead without benefit. Kanban let us pull new tasks as capacity opened, respond to scope changes immediately, and stay focused on finishing work rather than planning cycles.
How We Ran It
Each feature or screen was a card. Cards moved left to right: To Do when identified, In Progress when picked up, Done when merged and working. No card moved to Done unless it was functional and visible in the app.
Team Coordination
Each team member owned their In Progress cards. Check-ins happened naturally around blockers, not on a fixed cadence. This kept communication lightweight and let parallel work streams (frontend + backend) move independently.
Core Principles
01
Continuous Development
Work flows without time-boxed boundaries. Features ship as they are finished, not when a sprint ends. This matched the pace of a fast-moving class project.
02
Visualize the Workflow
The task board made work-in-progress visible to the whole team. Everyone could see what was blocked, what was moving, and where help was needed without a meeting.
03
Focus on Completion
Starting a new task before finishing an existing one was avoided. The goal was always to move cards to Done, keeping the board clean and the app stable at every checkpoint.

04 · Architectural Drift
What changed from
the original design, and why.
Backend integration in Sprint 2
Backend deferred to Sprint 3, mock data used throughout
Rationale: UX must be validated before real data introduces new variables. Deliberate, not reactive.
Tab-based navigation (bottom tab bar)
Stack navigation via Expo Router, header-to-screen push model
Rationale: Stack navigation felt more natural for secondary screens (Alerts, History, Settings). Clean back-button affordance without persistent tab bar.
Simple modal for dropdowns on Login
Animated bottom-sheet with spring physics, reused in Report modal and Settings dropdowns
Rationale: Peer testers found static modals abrupt. Animated sheet introduced in Sprint 1; became the standard pattern.
AsyncStorage for notification preferences
Local React state only, not persisted across sessions
Rationale: Persistence without a backend creates sync drift. Deferred until Firebase Auth exists so prefs are tied to a user account.
Key takeaway: All four drift points were deliberate trade-off decisions made in retrospectives, not unplanned scope creep. Architecture stayed coherent; the roadmap shifted to serve the UX.

06 · User Testing
Testing within the team, honest findings.
Testing was conducted internally by team members. As daily SDSU parking users ourselves, we brought genuine context to every session: we weren't pretending to be the user, we are the user.
Who Tested
Carlos Gans Charlie McMahon
QA led by Charlie, with Carlos cross-testing backend behavior against frontend output.
Approach
Each tester walked through the full app flow independently, noting where they hesitated, what confused them, and what felt natural. Findings documented and triaged against the Kanban board.
What worked well: color status system
Green / yellow / red was understood immediately with no explanation needed. The progress bar reinforced the status without adding complexity. Both testers confirmed this was the clearest part of the UI.
Confirmed. No changes needed. Design decision validated.
What worked well: report modal flow
The "I Arrived / I Left / Info looks wrong" options mapped directly to how testers naturally thought about updating parking data. The slide-up animation felt native and responsive.
Confirmed. Core crowd-reporting mechanic is intuitive and fast.
Confusion point: report modal context
Testers paused on "I Arrived / I Left" without structure context. "Arrived where, exactly?" was a common hesitation: the modal didn't show which lot it was tied to.
Fix: Added structure name to modal header to anchor the action clearly.
Usability issue: card tap expectation
Both testers expected tapping a parking structure card to open a map or directions, not the report modal. The primary tap action didn't match the mental model.
Added to Kanban To Do: primary tap opens map view; report modal moved to a secondary action.
